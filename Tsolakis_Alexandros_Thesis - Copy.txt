AbstractLiterature reviews require researchers to search multiple scholarly databases because publications are distributed across different platforms. Each database maintains distinct coverage and metadata standards. Researchers must query these systems individually, identify duplicate entries manually, and organize results before beginning actual analysis. With research output increasing roughly 4% per year, this fragmentation imposes growing time costs on the research process.This thesis describes AI Scholar, a system designed to integrate multiple scholarly databases through one search interface. The implementation uses asynchronous queries to contact databases simultaneously, reducing total search time compared to sequential approaches. Deduplication works by matching Digital Object Identifiers when available and comparing titles using fuzzy string matching for papers lacking identifiers. Results get ranked by combining several factors: how often papers have been cited, how recently they were published, and which database provided them. The system also connects to language models to generate brief summaries of paper content. The architecture separates database-specific code into provider modules, which convert different data formats into a standard model that other components can work with.The work shows that addressing database fragmentation doesn't require new technology. What already existsŃAPI access to databases, text processing algorithms, fuzzy matching techniquesŃcan be combined to produce a functional system. AI Scholar reduces the manual effort involved in multi-database literature searches by handling queries, removing duplicates, and organizing results automatically. Testing confirmed that this approach functions reliably for standard research workflows.

Introduction1.1 OverviewResearch output has grown tremendously in recent decades. This is not speculationŃit has been measured and documented through multiple bibliometric studies. Bornmann, Haunschild, and Mutz (2021) analyzed publication records from the major databasesŃScopus, Web of Science, Dimensions, and Microsoft AcademicŃand found the growth rate averages about 4.1 percent per year. At that rate, the total number of published papers doubles every 17 years or so. The growth isn't uniform across fields. Life Sciences sees about 5.1 percent annual growth, while Physical and Technical Sciences are closer to 5.5 percent. Depending on the academic discipline, it might be even more dramatic.For researchers trying to actually do a literature review, this creates a real problem. The issue isn't just the quantityŃit's that papers are scattered everywhere. Scopus (Elsevier, 2025) and Web of Science (Clarivate Analytics, 2025) are the main commercial databases. They have extensive coverage but cost institutions between $15,000 and $50,000 per year. They also restrict programmatic data access, requiring queries to be routed exclusively through their proprietary interfaces, Programmatic data extraction is restricted with code. Then there are the open platforms: arXiv (Cornell University, 2024) for preprints in physics, math, and computer science; CORE (CORE, 2024) which pulls together open-access papers from institutional repositories; OpenAlex (Priem, Piwowar, and Orr, 2022), which provides free access to metadata on millions of scholarly works; and CrossRef (Hendricks, Tkaczyk, Lin, and Feeney, 2020), which maintains standardized publication metadata. Each covers different papers. Each uses different metadata standards. Searching one database yields a specific set of results, while applying the same query to another platform frequently returns a different collection of papers. This is partly about what papers each database has indexed, partly about how their search algorithms rank results.What information scientists refer to as information overload is a significant component of the situation described here. Bawden and Robinson (2009) define information overload as a condition in which the volume, diversity, complexity, and dispersion of information impede rather than support effective cognition and decision-making. For the academic researches, this phenomenon manifests in concrete and recurring ways. Searches expanded in major databases such as Scopus may return hundreds of results, while similar or even the same queries in platforms like OpenAlex returned overlapping yet non-identical sets of publications. Researchers must then manually track retrieved papers, determine whether records across databases represent duplicates or distinct works, and organize the material into a usable system. These activities require substantial time investmentŃoften several hours per week for active researchers. Consequently, a significant portion of researchersŐ efforts is diverted toward administrative and organizational tasks, reducing the time available for critical reading, analysis, and engagement with core research questions.There's another problem too. Because different databases have different papers and rank them differently, researchers might be systematically missing relevant work. Or they might see a skewed sample depending on which database they happen to use most. The newer AI tools like Semantic Scholar (Allen Institute for Artificial Intelligence, 2024), ResearchRabbit (ResearchRabbit, 2024), and Connected Papers (Connected Papers, 2024) by displaying citation networks and suggesting related papers. But they operate independently from each other and from the main databases. They don't really solve the fragmentation problemŃthey just add more tools to the pile.But some things have improved. Open-access infrastructure has gotten substantially better over the last ten years. CORE now aggregates content from thousands of repositories and provides free Application Programming Interface (API) access, enabling programmatic querying (CORE, 2024). OpenAlex was specifically built as a free alternative to proprietary databases, with over 200 million papers indexed and APIs designed for researchers to use (Priem, Piwowar, and Orr, 2022). CrossRef keeps metadata on over 106 million publications and grows about 11 percent annually (Hendricks et al., 2020). These exist now. Five or ten years ago, this infrastructure didn't exist. The technical landscape has fundamentally changed.At the same time, AI and natural language processing got better. Text summarization algorithms can now capture the main findings and methods from a paper reasonably well, though they still struggle with highly technical language and specialized notation (Zhang, Zhao, Saleh, and Liu, 2023). Extracting key phrases and concepts from documents works with decent accuracy now. Large language models can understand academic text well enough to find papers that are conceptually similar, not just keyword matches (Brown, Mann, Ryder, et al., 2020). These capabilities didn't exist in their current form a few years ago. Put togetherŃthe availability of open data and these AI capabilitiesŃit becomes possible to actually build something useful.This thesis describes AI Scholar, a system that brings together these pieces. The platform searches across five major data sources simultaneously: arXiv (Cornell University, 2024), Semantic Scholar (Allen Institute for Artificial Intelligence, 2024), CrossRef (Hendricks et al., 2020), CORE (CORE, 2024), and OpenAlex (Priem, Piwowar, and Orr, 2022). It does three main things. First, it handles duplicates automaticallyŃwhen the same paper shows up in multiple databases, the system recognizes it's the same paper. Second, it ranks results sensibly instead of just using whatever ranking each database provides. Third, it summarizes papers and extracts key concepts to facilitate rapid comprehension of the content. The goal isn't to replace existing databases. It's to sit on top of themŃto provide one interface to query all of them at once and do the tedious work of organizing results automatically. Its design follows established software engineering practices, employing a layered architecture based on the ModelĐViewĐController (MVC) pattern. This approach was selected because it is well understood and supports extensibility, allowing new components to be introduced without disrupting existing functionality also it helps new developers to have a clean understanding of the code structure. The development process did not prioritize theoretical optimality; instead, it emphasized robustness and practical usability under real-world conditions. The underlying rationale is that systems which fail outside ideal circumstances offer limited value compared to those that produce acceptable results in imperfect and variable environments. Academic researchers operate across heterogeneous contexts, including differences in hardware, institutional access, and technical expertise. Consequently, a system capable of graceful degradation in the presence of errors or constraints is more valuable than a theoretically optimal system that is brittle in practice. This principle guided the overall design and implementation decisions.1.2 Aims and objectivesThe main goal of this thesis is to develop a system that helps researchers find papers more efficiently when conducting literature reviews. The secondary aim is to demonstrate that such a system can be built using infrastructure and tools that already exist, rather than requiring entirely new inventions. If this approach proves successful, it suggests the fragmentation problem described earlier is not unsolvableŃit is simply a matter of integrating existing resources.To accomplish these goals, three specific objectives have been established. The first objective is to design an integrative architecture capable of connecting to multiple databases and querying them simultaneously. This is more complex than it initially appears. The design must accommodate how each database functionsŃScopus (Elsevier, 2025), Web of Science (Clarivate Analytics, 2025), arXiv (Cornell University, 2024), CORE (CORE, 2024), OpenAlex (Priem, Piwowar, and Orr, 2022), and CrossRef (Hendricks, Tkaczyk, Lin, and Feeney, 2020)Ńand determine how to enable communication among them. Each platform maintains different data fields, different methods of representing identical information, and different capabilities. The architecture must identify commonalities across systems, manage differences appropriately, and present results to users without losing important contextual information about result origin. Success in this objective means achieving simultaneous querying of all five data sources and returning unified results that are comprehensible and actionable.The second objective involves implementing intelligent analysis layers on top of the unified data infrastructure. This encompasses three distinct components. First, automated deduplication mechanisms that identify when the same paper appears across multiple databasesŃan occurrence that happens frequently but inconsistently, requiring programmatic handling rather than manual intervention. This deduplication logic must recognize when two database records represent the same publication despite variations in formatting or nomenclature. Second, a relevance ranking system that prioritizes results sensibly. Individual databases employ different ranking algorithms based on their respective methodologies. The ranking system must integrate multiple factorsŃrelevance to the query, publication date, citation count, and source databaseŃrather than accepting the ranking provided by individual databases. Third, integration with large language model technologies to generate automated paper summaries and extract key concepts. When researchers receive search results, rapid comprehension of paper content without full-text reading is valuable. Machine-generated summaries facilitate this objective.The third objective is the technical verification of the system, ensuring that asynchronous queries function correctly, deduplication algorithms accurately merge overlapping records, and system latency remains within acceptable limits for interactive use. The implementation approach involves several sequential and different phases. The first phase consists of detailed analysis of each data source individuallyŃthe papers indexed, metadata organization, data collection, API capabilities, and limitations. Comprehensive documentation of these characteristics is necessary. The second phase involves architecture design capable of abstracting these differences and presenting a unified interface. A standard Model-View-Controller (MVC) architectural pattern will be used because it is well-established and permits modification or component addition without disrupting existing functionality. The third phase encompasses system implementationŃAPI integrations, deduplication algorithms, ranking system algorithm, and (LLM) Large Language Model integration. Testing occurs throughout to verify component functionality. An important clarification regarding system design: the objective is not to construct a theoretically perfect system. Rather, the goal is developing a system that functions effectively in real-world conditions while maintaining transparency regarding limitations. The deduplication mechanism will not identify every duplicate. The ranking algorithm will not prove optimal for all use cases. Comprehensive indexing of all existing papers is not feasible. Some relevant results may be missed because constituent data sources lack comprehensive coverage. However, a system that functions adequately for most researchers most of the time possesses greater practical value than a theoretically perfect system that functions poorly under realistic conditions. This pragmatic approach guides all design decisions.Upon completion, this thesis will produce three concrete deliverables. The first is a functional systemŃactual code and infrastructure available for researcher use. The second is comprehensive documentation explaining system construction, design decision rationale, accepted trade-offs, and system limitations. The third is an evaluation report demonstrating whether the system achieved stated objectivesŃspecifically whether it enables researchers to conduct superior literature reviews. These three deliverables collectively address whether the fragmentation problem is solvable using currently available infrastructure and technology.1.3 Research Questions and ScopeThe work presented in this thesis is organized around three research questions. The first part examines the difficulties researchers currently face when searching for academic papers across multiple databases. The second part explains the system being developed and how it addresses those difficulties. The chapter also defines what the project can realistically accomplish given the available resources, and evaluates whether the work is feasible from financial, technical, and time perspectives. 2.2 MotivationResearchers today face a frustrating situation that has been building for decades. The number of scientific papers published each year keeps growing at documented ratesŃBornmann, Haunschild, and Mutz (2021) found annual growth averaging 4.1 percent across major databases. This means the total volume of published research doubles roughly every 17 years. For someone starting a PhD in 2026, there are literally twice as many papers in their field as there were when their advisor started. That growth alone would not be such a problem if everything were organized in one place. The real issue is that papers are scattered across dozens of different websites and databases, each with its own search interface, metadata standards, and coverage areas.For a researcher who wants to do a thorough literature review, the current workflow looks like this. They have to search in arXiv because that is where the preprints are, especially for computer science, physics, and mathematics (Cornell University, 2024). Then they have to check Semantic Scholar because it offers AI-powered paper recommendations and citation analysis (Allen Institute for Artificial Intelligence, 2024). They may then query CrossRef for standardized metadata and DOIs (Hendricks, Tkaczyk, Lin, and Feeney, 2020), CORE for open-access papers from institutional repositories (CORE, 2024), and OpenAlex if comprehensive coverage including citations and author affiliations is required (Priem, Piwowar, and Orr, 2022). Each time, the researcher must reformulate their search terms slightly differently because each platformŐs search syntax operates differently. Each database returns results in different formats with different metadata fields, and each one ranks results according to its own algorithm.This fragmentation creates concrete problems. First is simple inefficiency. Searching five databases manually takes timeŃeasily an hour or more for a comprehensive search on a new topic. Second is the duplication problem. The same paper often appears in multiple databases, but with slightly different metadata. One database might contain the preprint version from arXiv, another the published version with a Digital Object Identifiers (DOIs) from CrossRef, and a third citation data from Semantic Scholar. A researcher may end up with the same paper listed multiple times in their reference manager unless duplicates are identified and removed manually. Third is incompleteness. No single database has everything. A researcher might find fifteen relevant papers in Semantic Scholar, twelve in arXiv, and then discover three critical papers in CORE that did not appear anywhere else. There is no clear point at which they can be certain that all relevant papers have been identified. The honest answer is that they cannot.Bawden and Robinson (2009) described this phenomenon as information overloadŃwhen the volume and dispersion of information actually impedes rather than supports effective decision-making. In research contexts, this manifests in what information scientists call Ňsatisficing behavior.Ó Researchers do not search exhaustively across all available databases because doing so would require too much time. Instead, they search until they find ŇenoughÓ papers and then stop, even while recognizing that relevant work has probably been missed. This is rational behavior under time constraints, but it is not ideal for research quality. The fragmentation of databases creates artificial barriers that stem from information system design rather than from the intrinsic difficulty of the research problems themselves.Newer AI-enhanced tools like Semantic Scholar, ResearchRabbit (ResearchRabbit, 2024), and Connected Papers (Connected Papers, 2024) have attempted to address parts of this problem. They display citation networks, suggest related papers, and use machine learning techniques to identify conceptual similarities between works. These are genuine improvements. However, they do not resolve the fundamental issue of fragmentation. Each operates as an independent platform with its own data sources and interface. A researcher using these tools still has to consult multiple systems and manually integrate the results. The tools add valuable functionality, but they also add more separate environments that must be learned and managed.In recent years, both the underlying infrastructure and the available technology have improved substantially. On the infrastructure side, major open-access platforms now provide programmatic access through well-documented APIs. CORE aggregates content from thousands of institutional repositories and makes it freely queryable (CORE, 2024). OpenAlex was specifically designed as a free alternative to proprietary citation databases, with over 200 million works indexed and APIs built for researcher use (Priem, Piwowar, and Orr, 2022). CrossRef maintains metadata on over 106 million publications and grows by about 11 percent annually (Hendricks et al., 2020). Five or ten years ago, this infrastructure did not exist in such an accessible form. Proprietary databases were the only option for comprehensive coverage, and they typically did not provide convenient API access except under expensive institutional licensing arrangements.On the technology side, artificial intelligence and natural language processing have reached a point where they can genuinely support research tasks rather than serving only as experimental demonstrations. Text summarization algorithms can now capture the main findings and methods of a paper with reasonable accuracy, although they continue to struggle with highly technical notation and specialized terminology (Zhang, Zhao, Saleh, and Liu, 2023). Large language models can interpret academic text well enough to identify conceptually similar papers rather than relying solely on keyword overlap (Brown, Mann, Ryder, et al., 2020). These models can extract key concepts from abstracts, identify research questions, and summarize methodologies. Their output is not perfectŃno responsible researcher would base critical decisions only on AI-generated summariesŃbut it is sufficiently reliable to help researchers quickly assess whether a paper merits close reading.Taken togetherŃfreely accessible data through APIs and AI models capable of understanding academic textŃthese developments make it technically feasible to build something that previously was not realistic. It becomes possible to query multiple databases simultaneously through a single interface, to automatically deduplicate results by comparing metadata fields intelligently, to rank papers using multiple criteria beyond what any single database provides, and to generate summaries that help researchers understand content faster. None of this requires fundamental breakthroughs in theory. The infrastructure exists; the AI models exist. What is required is thoughtful integration.The motivation for AI Scholar arises directly from this context. The primary goal is to save researchers time by providing a single search interface instead of many. Rather than manually querying arXiv, then Semantic Scholar, then CrossRef, then CORE, the researcher issues one query and the system handles all of them. Rather than manually checking for duplicates across result sets, the system identifies when different records represent the same paper. Rather than reading every abstract to understand what each paper is about, the researcher can consult AI-generated summaries that highlight key findings. The underlying databases remain the authoritative sources; AI Scholar does not attempt to replace them. Instead, it functions as an aggregation layer that handles the repetitive work of searching, deduplicating, and organizing.A secondary motivation is to demonstrate that addressing this type of problem does not require massive institutional investment. The necessary tools already exist, and the data is accessible. What is needed is careful integration and design. If AI Scholar proves effective in helping researchers conduct higher-quality literature reviews in less time, it will suggest that many research infrastructure challenges are tractable at relatively small scale. There is no need to wait for large publishers or universities to implement such solutions. Individual researchers or small teams can address meaningful gaps using currently available resources.This is what motivates the present work. The problem is real and well documented. The opportunity to address it has been created by recent developments in open infrastructure and AI capabilities. The proposed solution is feasible without requiring breakthroughs. If successful, it will provide both a useful tool and a concrete demonstration that thoughtful integration of existing resources can meaningfully improve research practice.2.2.1 Existing System DescriptionThe process of searching academic literature rarely happens in one centralized place. Instead, researchers navigate a scattered collection of different platforms. The foundation of this setup usually relies on massive bibliographic databases like Scopus and Web of Science. These platforms hold a vast amount of peer-reviewed journals, conference papers, and other scholarly work, though accessing them usually requires a university subscription, and their interfaces can be quite complex (Elsevier, 2025; Clarivate Analytics, 2025). Alongside these commercial giants, subject-specific repositories like arXiv are essential in fields such as computer science and physics, offering an open-access route to preprints before they are officially published (Cornell University, 2024).Filling in the gaps are open metadata and aggregation services. CrossRef is a major player here, handling the metadata and persistent identifiers (like DOIs) that keep links reliable and ensure different versions of a paper are properly connected (Hendricks, Tkaczyk, Lin, and Feeney, 2020). CORE gathers open-access content from institutional repositories globally, while OpenAlex provides a massive, openly licensed index of authors, works, and institutions, complete with public APIs (CORE, 2024; Priem, Piwowar, and Orr, 2022). Because each of these systems was built to solve a slightly different problemŃwhether that is citation tracking, harvesting repositories, or providing open infrastructureŃtheir coverage, interface design, and the depth of their metadata vary significantly.Recently, a wave of AI-enhanced discovery tools has also entered the picture. Platforms like Semantic Scholar, ResearchRabbit, and Connected Papers take existing bibliographic data and add features like citation graphs, automated recommendations, and tools to identify influential papers (Allen Institute for Artificial Intelligence, 2024; ResearchRabbit, 2024; Connected Papers, 2024). While these tools are genuinely useful for mapping out a specific topic or citation network, they ultimately represent just another set of separate systems that researchers have to figure out how to incorporate into their daily routines.Workflow Fragmentation and Integration ChallengesFor a researcher attempting to conduct a systematic literature review, this fragmented landscape turns the process into a multi-step chore. A typical workflow often involves querying Scopus and Web of Science for established literature, checking arXiv for the newest preprints, using CrossRef to verify DOIs, and then digging into CORE or OpenAlex to track down open-access versions or fill in missing data (Elsevier, 2025; Clarivate Analytics, 2025; Cornell University, 2024; CORE, 2024; Priem, Piwowar, and Orr, 2022). After all that, AI tools might be used to expand the search. The fundamental problem is that every single platform requires the search query to be rewritten to match its specific syntax, and the results are returned in completely different formats, ranked by different criteria.Since these platforms are not designed to talk to each other, the researcher has to handle all the integration manually. Records must be exported from each site, imported into a reference manager, and then painstakingly checked for duplicates. Because details like author names, titles, or page numbers often vary slightly between databases, the automated deduplication tools built into reference managers frequently fail, forcing manual inspection. Moreover, if specific citation counts or impact metrics are needed, researchers usually have to go back and manually cross-reference papers in Scopus or Web of Science, as the other platforms rarely track that specific data (Elsevier, 2025; Clarivate Analytics, 2025).The current implementations nowadays are not a cohesive "system" at all, but a patchwork of partially overlapping services that forces the user to manage a significant amount of coordination. Each platform works well for its intended purpose, but their separation means that conducting a thorough literature search requires constantly switching interfaces, re-running queries, and manually fixing duplicate records. This is precisely the problem AI Scholar is designed to address. The goal is not to replace these established databases, but to build an integration layer that eliminates the busywork involved in navigating such a fragmented environment.2.2.2 Project ScopeAI Scholar is defined as a browserbased research support system that aggregates results from multiple scholarly data providers into a single, coherent interface and applies lightweight AI assistance on top of this aggregated view. The application is intended for individual researchersŃprimarily postgraduate students and academic staffŃwho conduct literature reviews and exploratory searches as part of their everyday work. It is not designed as an institutional repository, publishing platform, or fullscale library system, but as a focused tool that reduces the overhead associated with searching and organizing scientific publications across fragmented sources.?Functionally, the system targets five primary data providers: arXiv for preprints in fields such as computer science and physics, Semantic Scholar for enriched metadata and AIaugmented discovery, CrossRef for DOIcentric bibliographic records, CORE for aggregated openaccess content, and OpenAlex for broad, open bibliographic coverage (Cornell University, 2024; Allen Institute for Artificial Intelligence, 2024; Hendricks, Tkaczyk, Lin, and Feeney, 2020; CORE, 2024; Priem, Piwowar, and Orr, 2022). For each user query, AI Scholar issues parallel requests to the selected providers, collects their responses, and normalizes them into a common internal representation. Records that refer to the same underlying publicationŃidentified by shared DOIs, arXiv identifiers, or, where necessary, fuzzy matching over titles and authorsŃare merged into a single consolidated entry.?Within this aggregated result set, the system applies a ranking process that combines traditional signals, such as publication date and citation count where available, with relevance scores produced by a large language model operating over titles and abstracts. This hybrid ranking is designed to surface papers that are both conceptually aligned with the query and of clear scholarly significance, while still allowing users to resort by simpler criteria such as date or provider when needed. On demand, the system can request AIgenerated summaries and key point extractions for individual papers via external providers such as Google Gemini and OpenRouter, and present these alongside the original metadata to support rapid assessment of relevance.?At the user level, the scope includes account management, private search histories, and personal collections of saved papers. Each authenticated user has an individual workspace in which previous searches are stored, together with cached views of past result sets, so that earlier queries can be revisited without repeatedly querying all external APIs. From any result list or collection, selected records can be exported in standard formats such as BibTeX, RIS, or CSV for use with established reference managers (for example, Zotero, Mendeley, or EndNote). The system is delivered as a web application optimised for desktop and laptop browsers and is intended to be deployable on modest infrastructure, such as a single PythonAnywhere instance or a small Dockerbased server.?Equally important are the boundaries of the project. AI Scholar does not host fulltext articles; it operates exclusively on metadata and abstracts obtained from the upstream providers and redirects users to the original sources for complete content. It deliberately avoids implementing behavioral recommendation featuresŃsuch as suggesting new papers based on a userŐs reading historyŃor social functions like shared workspaces, commenting, or following other researchers. Mobile applications, realtime collaboration, and largescale institutional deployment are explicitly outside the targeted scope. By constraining itself to multisource search, aggregation, deduplication, ranking, ondemand summarization, and basic personal organization features, the project focuses on solving a clearly defined and technically tractable part of the broader discovery problem rather than attempting to replace existing scholarly platforms.? 2.2.3 Performance/Behavior RequirementsThe performance requirements for AI Scholar follow directly from its role as an aggregation layer over multiple external services rather than as a standalone database. The endtoend response time of a search operation is therefore constrained by the slowest upstream provider participating in that query. Under normal conditions, a complete search across all five target sourcesŃarXiv, Semantic Scholar, CrossRef, CORE, and OpenAlexŃshould return an initial result list within a few seconds, with a soft target of under ten seconds for typical queries on a standard university network connection. When one or more providers responds slowly or becomes temporarily unavailable, the system is expected to degrade gracefully by displaying partial results together with clear indicators of which sources have not yet responded or are offline.?From a concurrency perspective, the system is intended to support a modest number of simultaneous users typical of a research group or small department rather than institutionwide deployment. The target is to sustain concurrent use by a few dozen active users performing intermittent searches without noticeable degradation in perceived responsiveness. To achieve this, requests to external APIs are issued asynchronously and, where feasible, shared across identical queries so that multiple users issuing the same search term within a short interval do not each trigger independent upstream calls. Internally, frequently accessed data such as recent search results and AIgenerated summaries are cached for limited periods to reduce redundant computation and external traffic, while ensuring that longterm consistency is maintained by allowing users to rerun searches explicitly when uptodate results are required.?The behavior of the AI components is also subject to explicit constraints. Summaries and relevance scores are requested on demand rather than precomputed for all results, in order to stay within rate limits and cost boundaries imposed by external AI providers such as Google Gemini and OpenRouter. Once generated, these summaries are stored and reused for subsequent views of the same paper, avoiding repeated calls for identical content. If an AI request fails or exceeds quota, the system is required to fall back to presenting the original metadata and abstract without causing the surrounding interface to fail. The overall expectation is that the absence of AIgenerated enhancements should never prevent core search and browsing functionality from operating correctly.?On the client side, the interface is expected to remain responsive when displaying typical result set sizes of up to a few hundred records. Pagination and incremental loading are used to avoid rendering all results at once, and clientside filtering and sorting are implemented in a way that does not introduce noticeable lag on commonly available hardware. Error conditionsŃincluding timeouts, missing fields in upstream metadata, or partial failures of individual providersŃare handled by nondisruptive notifications within the interface rather than modal error dialogs, so that researchers can continue to work with whatever information is currently available. Collectively, these performance and behavioral requirements define a system that is not optimized for largescale, highthroughput operation, but for reliable, Ňgood enoughÓ responsiveness and robustness in the smallteam, academic usage scenarios for which AI Scholar is intended.?2.2.4  Technical ConstrainsFundamentally, AI Scholar functions as an overlay on several external services, meaning its technical boundaries are inherited from those third-party dependencies rather than stemming from limitations in its own architecture. The platform pulls data directly from arXiv, Semantic Scholar, CrossRef, CORE, and OpenAlex via their public APIs. As is standard with open infrastructure, these providers impose strict rate limits and usage policies to throttle high-frequency automated queries.To navigate these bottlenecks, the system implements query batching and caches recent searches. The architecture is intentionally designed for moderate, rather than institutional-scale, traffic. Provided the tool is deployed within a typical small research group or department, these external constraints remain highly manageable and will not disrupt the intended workflow.The AI components introduce an additional set of practical considerations. Providers such as Google Gemini and OpenRouter expose powerful language models through metered APIs, typically with free tiers and payasyougo extensions. This project assumes access to such services within modest budgetary constraints and therefore treats AI functionalityŃsummarisation and relevance scoringŃas an enhancement rather than a hard dependency for basic search and aggregation. Summaries are generated on demand and cached, and the system is explicitly designed to continue operating with full core functionality if AI calls are temporarily unavailable, ratelimited, or intentionally disabled. In this sense, the technical constraints of AI usage shape implementation strategies and user experience design, but they do not undermine the viability of the platform as a whole.?Framework and infrastructure choices also play a role. The web application is implemented using Flask and a small set of wellestablished extensions, which is appropriate for a researchoriented prototype but implies that features common in large production systemsŃsuch as integrated role management, centralised logging at scale, or sophisticated horizontal scaling mechanismsŃare not primary design targets. Instead, the architecture prioritises clarity, modularity, and ease of deployment on modest infrastructure, such as a single virtual machine or managed Python hosting service. This is well aligned with the projectŐs scope: support for small numbers of concurrent users and straightforward reproducibility by other researchers are valued more highly than the ability to serve thousands of users simultaneously.?Finally, the system must accommodate variability in upstream metadata quality. Some APIs return rich, consistent records that include abstracts, identifiers, and citation metrics, while others provide only minimal bibliographic fields. AI Scholar addresses this by normalising fields where possible, tolerating missing values, and presenting whatever information is available without failing when certain attributes are absent. These technical constraints do not constitute fundamental obstacles; rather, they inform a pragmatic architectural approach that emphasises modular integration, graceful degradation, and realistic expectations about what can be achieved within the resource and dependency envelope of an academic software project.2.3 ĘFeasibility StudyThis section assesses the overall feasibility of the AI Scholar project by considering the available resources, the technical environment, and the time limits of the dissertation. This section reviews related literature in four areas relevant to the systemŐs design: (i) academic search and aggregation across multiple data sources, (ii) AIassisted tools for scholarly discovery, (iii) bibliographic deduplication and record linkage, and (iv) ranking approaches for scientific literature. The aim is to synthesise the main findings and design patterns reported in prior studies, and to clarify how the present work builds on this evidence while addressing remaining limitations in current practice.2.4.1 Academic Search  The challenge of searching across multiple academic databases has been a persistent problem in scholarly research. When Gusenbauer and Haddaway (2020) set out to evaluate which search systems were actually suitable for systematic reviews, they tested 28 platforms ranging from Google Scholar to PubMed and Web of Science. The approach was simple enough: run the same search on every platform and see how well each one handled Boolean operators and other standard search techniques that researchers depend on. What they found was concerning. Fewer than half of the platforms tested met basic standards for rigorous evidence synthesis. Google Scholar, despite indexing an enormous volume of literature, turned out to be particularly unsuitable for systematic reviewsŃnot because of coverage issues, but because it provides weak support for Boolean searching and makes it nearly impossible to reproduce the same results twice.?The problem is obvious enough that several research teams have attempted to solve it through aggregation. Xu et al. (2020) built a cloud-based system that distributes a single query to multiple academic search engines, waits for responses, and then applies clustering algorithms to organise everything that comes back. It workedŃthe prototype did reduce how much time researchers spent manually sorting through papersŃbut the implementation was limited. It only connected to a handful of databases, and the duplicate detection was basic at best.?A more sophisticated attempt came from Dobrescu et al. (2023), who developed the Aggregated Research Retrieval System (ARRS). ARRS queries a broader set of sources: IEEE, Scopus, CrossRef, arXiv, and ResearchGate. The architecture is hybrid, meaning some content is pre-indexed and stored in local databases for speed, while other content is retrieved on demand through API calls or web scraping. Apache Spark handles the parallel processing, and citation networks are stored in graph databases to keep query performance reasonable even when result sets grow large. This design is conceptually similar to what AI Scholar does, though ARRS stops short of incorporating AI for summarisation or relevance scoring.?What these systems demonstrate is that querying multiple databases from a single interface is feasible and genuinely helpful. What they also demonstrate is where the hard problems remain. Response times degrade under load. Metadata quality is inconsistent across sourcesŃsometimes wildly so. Result ranking becomes arbitrary when different databases use fundamentally different scoring algorithms. AI Scholar tackles these specific issues by integrating AI-based ranking and summarisation on top of the aggregation layer, rather than treating metadata merging as the endpoint.2.4.2 AI-Enhanced Scholarly Discovery ToolsWhen Semantic Scholar launched about a decade ago, it represented one of the first serious attempts to apply deep learning to academic search at scale. The Allen Institute for Artificial Intelligence built the platform around neural embeddingsŃspecifically Bidirectional Encoder Representations from Transformers (BERT) based encodings of paper abstracts paired with citation graph structureŃto move past the limitations of keyword matching (Allen Institute for Artificial Intelligence, 2024). The idea was straightforward: if a paper is encoded what a paper is about rather than just indexing the words it contains, it can be identified conceptually similar work even when authors use completely different terminology. For exploratory research, the general area might be known but not the precise terms people use, this approach works considerably better than traditional Boolean search.Not all AI-enhanced systems are equally forthcoming about how they work. Khraisha and Put (Liu, Y., Sullivan, P., & Sinnamon, L. (2024). AI Transparency in Academic Search Systems: An Initial Exploration) examined ten widely used academic search platforms to assess their transparency around AI mechanisms. Five disclosed reasonable detail about their algorithms. Three provided partial information. Two offered essentially no explanation of how they ranked or recommended papers. This opacity creates real problems for researchers who need to document their search strategies, particularly in systematic reviews where reproducibility and comprehensiveness are requirements rather than preferences. One design goal for AI Scholar was avoiding this black-box problem by documenting the ranking logic and stating explicitly which models handle which tasks.?Citation network tools represent a different category of AI-assisted discovery. ResearchRabbit, Litmaps, and Connected Papers all build on the idea that relationships between papersŃwho cites whom, which works appear together in bibliographiesŃreveal conceptual connections that keyword searches miss (ResearchRabbit, 2024; Connected Papers, 2024). ResearchRabbit calls its method "rabbit hole" searching: you provide a seed paper, it identifies highly connected citations in the local network, and you iteratively explore outward from there. Litmaps uses SPECTER embeddings to calculate semantic similarity between titles and abstracts, then arranges results chronologically so you can trace how particular ideas developed. Both tools are genuinely useful for exploration. Neither addresses the core issue that motivated this thesis: researchers must still query multiple separate databases to avoid missing relevant literature.Generative AI has also been tested for literature search workflows, with mixed results. Wang et al. (2024) evaluated whether ChatGPT could formulate search strings and screen papers for systematic reviews. The answer was sometimes, but inconsistently, and errors appeared frequently enough that manual verification remained necessary. Recent evaluations of GPT-4 in systematic review screening have found that while the model significantly increases processing speed, it occasionally misinterprets inclusion criteria due to a lack of deep contextual understanding compared to human reviewers (Kusunoki et al., 2024). These findings suggest AI can support but not replace human expertise in literature searchŃa principle that shapes how AI Scholar uses summarisation, treating it as a screening aid rather than a substitute for reading the source material.2.4.3 Bibliographic Deduplication TechniquesIdentifying when two database records refer to the same publication is harder than it sounds. The same paper can appear in multiple databases with different formattingŃone might store the title in sentence case, another in title case; author names might be listed as "Smith, J." in one system and "John Smith" in another; publication dates can differ if one database records the preprint version and another records the journal version. For systematic reviews, where completeness and accuracy are essential, failing to detect duplicates means either counting the same paper twice or spending hours manually checking records.Jiang et al. (2014) tackled this with a two-stage rule-based algorithm. The first stage groups records by publication year to reduce the number of pairwise comparisons neededŃComparing publications across disparate decades is computationally inefficient. The second stage applies denial rules and fuzzy text matching to identify duplicates within each year group. Their method combined exact matching on structured fields like DOIs with approximate string similarity for titles and authors, using a threshold of 0.8 for the similarity score. The results were strong: 100% precision and 95% recall. What made it work was not relying solely on any single field. Titles alone fail too often because of formatting differences. DOIs work when present, but many records lack them, especially for older papers or preprints.?Large-scale aggregation systems face even steeper challenges. OpenAIRE aggregates metadata from thousands of repositories worldwide, which means dealing with every possible variation in how institutions format bibliographic data (Manghi et al., 2018). Their deduplication algorithms compare titles, publication years, and author strings, but titles alone produce too many false negatives. Differences in punctuation, capitalisation, transliteration from non-Latin scripts, and handling of special characters all create problems. OpenAIRE addresses this through canonicalisation: convert everything to lowercase, strip punctuation, normalise whitespace, and then compare. It is not perfect, but it reduces the error rate substantially.?Nguyen et al. (2015) approached the problem differently using fingerprint-based methods and n-gram language models. Instead of comparing individual fields, their system extracts socio-semantic features from recordsŃpatterns in how authors, venues, and topics co-occurŃand applies collocation-based similarity measures. The method achieved recall above 95% while maintaining 100% precision, which is notable for heterogeneous bibliographic corpora where traditional field-matching struggles. The trade-off is computational cost: generating fingerprints and computing n-gram similarities is slower than simple string matching.?AI Scholar borrows from all three approaches. Records are first matched on DOIs and arXiv identifiers when available, which provides perfect accuracy at essentially zero cost. For records without persistent identifiers, the system applies fuzzy matching on titles and authors using a weighted similarity threshold. Finally, when multiple records are determined to represent the same paper, their metadata is merged to produce a consolidated entry that combines information from all sourcesŃpublication dates, citation counts, abstracts, and URLs.2.4.4 Ranking and Recommendation in Scientific LiteratureHow search systems order results has direct consequences for whether researchers find what they need. Traditional information retrieval relies heavily on term frequencyĐinverse document frequency (TF-IDF) and related statistical measures, which perform adequately for general web search but encounter difficulties in academic contexts. Conceptual similarity between papers often has little to do with shared keywords. Two papers addressing the same research question might use entirely different vocabulary depending on their disciplinary origins or methodological approaches.Liu, Kou, Hou, Lin, and Yang (2015, 2016) reframed scientific paper recommendation as a link prediction problem, incorporating temporal factors, author influence metrics, and citation network structure to identify collections of papers that collectively address a user's information requirements. Their work recognises something fundamental: researchers do not evaluate recommendations as isolated items but as sets. A recommendation system that suggests five individually relevant papers that all cover the same narrow subtopic is less useful than one that suggests five papers spanning the breadth of a research question, even if each individual paper is marginally less relevant. This insight has implications for multi-source aggregation, where the goal is assembling a comprehensive view rather than optimising for the single highest-scoring result.?Sakib, Khaled, Hasan, and Islam (2020) developed methods that exploit second-level citation informationŃpatterns in which papers are cited togetherŃrather than depending on explicit user profiles or preference data. By measuring similarity through co-citation analysis, their system identifies correlated publications that share conceptual connections without necessarily sharing vocabulary. The approach combines content-based features extracted from titles, keywords, and abstracts with collaborative filtering derived from citation networks. This hybrid strategy addresses a limitation of purely content-based methods: papers can be highly relevant to each other without using the same terminology, and citation behaviour reveals these latent connections.?Recent work has explored federating multiple embedding methods for academic search. R‡cz, Vandecappelle, Knoth, and Collins (2024) introduced Scholar API (SAPI), an infrastructure that supports multiple neural and linear-algebra-based embeddings including SPECTER, ProNE-s, SciNCL, and graph neural network representations. SAPI's architecture allows researchers to combine solutions from different embedding models and select among various approaches depending on task requirements. This flexibility parallels AI Scholar's multi-provider AI strategy, which maintains fallback mechanisms across different language model services to ensure availability when individual providers experience rate limiting or outages.AI Scholar's ranking mechanism draws on these precedent approaches while prioritising interpretability. The system combines citation count, publication recency, source database confidence scores, and AI-generated relevance assessments to produce a hybrid ranking. Unlike systems that rely exclusively on opaque neural embeddings, this design permits users to understand why particular papers rank highly. Each ranking factor is weighted and documented, which supports both reproducibility and debugging when results appear unexpected.2.4.5 Gaps In Existing Research Despite substantial progress in each of these areas, a clear gap remains: no widely available system integrates multi-source aggregation, intelligent deduplication, AI-powered summarisation, and hybrid ranking within a single, accessible platform designed for individual researchers. Existing aggregation systems either lack AI assistance (Xu et al., 2020; Dobrescu et al., 2023) or operate as proprietary services within commercial platforms (Scopus AI, Web of Science Research Assistant) that are not freely accessible and do not support multi-database federation beyond their own indexes.AI-enhanced discovery tools such as Semantic Scholar, ResearchRabbit, and Connected Papers provide valuable recommendation and visualisation features but do not address the core fragmentation problem: researchers must still consult multiple platforms to ensure comprehensive coverage (Allen Institute for Artificial Intelligence, 2024; ResearchRabbit, 2024; Connected Papers, 2024). Furthermore, these tools generally do not generate on-demand summaries or provide transparent, customisable ranking mechanisms.AI Scholar is positioned to fill this gap by combining the aggregation approach of systems like ARRS with the AI-assisted relevance and summarisation capabilities demonstrated in recent research, while maintaining transparency, modularity, and accessibility. It is therefore important to identify the main risks and to make explicit the assumptions under which the system is designed and evaluated.?A first group of risks is associated with dependence on external services. Core functionality relies on scholarly data providers for metadata and abstracts and on large language model services for summaries. Changes in rate limits, access policies, or response formats at arXiv, Semantic Scholar, CrossRef, CORE, OpenAlex, or the AI providers could affect performance or temporarily reduce functionality. The architecture mitigates this by isolating all external integrations in a provider layer, using caching to reduce repeated calls, and implementing fallbacks where partial results are still displayed when one source is unavailable. The project assumes that public API access to these services remains broadly stable and that free or lowcost tiers continue to be sufficient for the modest usage expected during development and evaluation.?A second category of risk concerns data quality and consistency. Metadata returned by different providers varies in completeness and formatting, which can influence deduplication, ranking, and the usefulness of summaries. AI Scholar includes mechanisms for normalising fields, merging records that refer to the same publication, and tolerating missing values, but these mechanisms rely on the assumption that most returned metadata is at least minimally coherent. In domains where records are particularly sparse or inconsistent, additional domainspecific cleaning rules might be required, which is beyond the scope of the present implementation.?There are also risks related to the behaviour of large language models. Summaries and relevance explanations generated from titles and abstracts may occasionally be incomplete, imprecise, or overly confident. The system addresses this by keeping AIgenerated content brief, by always presenting the original metadata and abstract alongside any summary, and by treating AI output as an aid for initial screening rather than as an authoritative source. The evaluation assumes that researchers will use these summaries to prioritise reading rather than to replace direct engagement with the underlying papers.?Finally, projectmanagement risks arise from the fact that AI Scholar is developed by a single person within a fixed academic period. Unexpected personal circumstances, technical difficulties, or underestimation of implementation effort could affect the completeness of the final system. To manage this, the work plan assumes that core featuresŃmultisource search, aggregation, deduplication, and basic rankingŃare prioritised, while nonessential enhancements can be deferred or simplified if necessary. First, why run search providers in parallel rather than sequentially? Network latency. If five APIs are queried sequentially and each takes one second, that's five seconds minimum. Users notice the difference.Second, why normalize everything to a common Paper format immediately in providers rather than keeping raw API responses? Separating AIService lets SearchService work without it, and enables independent testing and modification of AI features.Fourth, why cache at the service layer rather than at the provider layer? It emerged from actually building the system and running into problems. The current structure reflects what worked when APIs went down, when ranking algorithms changed, when new databases got added. Understanding these pieces matters if the system needs modification or debugging later.3.3.1 Controller Layer  Controllers handle HTTP requests. Their job is straightforward: receive a request, validate it, call the appropriate service, return a response. They don't implement business logic. This isn't arbitraryŃit's a deliberate design choice. If business logic gets embedded in controllers, logic can't be testedc without spinning up the entire web server. It  also can't be reused it in other contexts like command-line scripts or background jobs. So controllers stay thin by design. SearchController is the main entry point for search requests. It exposes /api/search as a POST endpoint. When a request comes in, the controller does basic validation. Is the query string empty? Which databases did the user select? Are there any filters like publication year or author name? Once the inputs check out, it calls SearchService.search() with those parameters. The service does the actual work. When results come back, SearchController wraps them in JSON with pagination informationŃhow many total results, which page this is, how many results per page. If something goes wrongŃmalformed request, server errorŃthe controller returns an appropriate status code (400 for bad requests, 500 for server errors) and an error message. PaperController handles individual paper operations. The main endpoint is /api/paper/{id}, which returns full details about a specific paperŃabstract, authors with affiliations, citation count, related papers unless having them. There's also /api/paper/{id}/cite which generates formatted citations in different styles (APA, MLA, Chicago). The controller validates the paper ID, calls PaperService.get_paper_details(id), and returns what it gets back. If the paper doesn't exist, it returns 404. WebController serves HTML pages rather than JSON. It handles routes like / for the homepage, /search for the search interface, /about for documentation. Each route loads a template, passes in whatever data that template needs, returns rendered HTML. This controller is simpler than the others because most of the dynamic behavior happens client-side in JavaScript. AuthController manages authentication and sessions. It provides /api/auth/login for authentication, /api/auth/logout for ending sessions, and /api/auth/verify for checking whether a session is still valid. Authentication is optionalŃguests can search without logging in. Authenticated users get additional features like saved searches and history. The implementation uses JSON Web Tokens (JWT). Each token contains user information and is cryptographically signed, which lets the server verify it without querying the database every time. ? The controller pattern keeps the codebase organized. Every HTTP endpoint corresponds to one controller method. Controllers are shortŃusually 20-30 lines that validate input, call a service, format the response. This makes them easy to test because there's not much logic to verify. It's also easy to see what API endpoints exist just by looking at the controller classes.3.3.2 Service LayerService components implement the principal business logic of the system. In contrast to controllers, which are directly bound to the HTTP layer, services are designed as independent Python classes that can be executed in multiple contexts. They may be invoked within web requests, from command-line interfaces, by background processing tasks, or during unit testing. This separation of concerns strengthens modularity and enhances the overall adaptability of the architecture.The SearchService is responsible for managing the complete search procedure. Its central method, search(query, options), receives a query string together with optional configuration parameters, including the databases to be queried, filtering constraints for the results, and an option to enable AI-driven summarization. The method proceeds through a sequence of coordinated steps, each addressing a specific stage of the retrieval and processing workflow, thereby structuring the search operation into clearly defined phases.First, it figures out which search providers to query based on options.databases. If the user picked specific ones, use those. Otherwise query all five. Then it creates asynchronous tasks for each provider using Python's asyncio. This runs queries in parallel instead of one after another. Each task calls the provider's search() method.The service waits for all tasks to complete, or times out after 5 seconds, whichever comes first. If a provider is slow, the whole search doesn't hang. Results from all providers get collected into one list. At this point there are duplicatesŃthe same paper might appear from multiple databases.The subsequent stage involves invoking PaperService.deduplicate() in order to detect and consolidate duplicate records within the retrieved dataset. Once redundancy has been addressed, RankingService.rank() is applied to evaluate and order the resulting set according to predefined scoring criteria.When AI-based summarization is activated, the system calls AIService.generate_summaries() for the top ranked items. This operation is executed asynchronously, allowing the primary search results to be returned without delay while summaries are generated in the background.The process concludes with the return of the ranked results, accompanied by metadata indicating the distribution of records across the queried databases.SearchService also implements caching. If the same query gets searched twice within a short period, it returns cached results instead of hitting APIs again. Cache keys are based on the query string and selected options. Cache entries expire after 15 minutes because search results can change as new papers get indexed.AIService manages all interactions with language models. It has several methods: generate_summary(text) creates a concise summary of a paper abstract, score_relevance(query, abstract) returns a 0-1 score indicating how well a paper matches the query, extract_keywords(text) identifies important terms.The challenge with AI services is reliability. Language model APIs have rate limits, sometimes go down, occasionally return errors. AIService handles this through a provider fallback mechanism. It tries GeminiProvider first (Google's Gemini API). If that fails due to rate limiting or network issues, it falls back to OpenRouterProvider which aggregates multiple models. If both fail, it returns gracefully without crashing the searchŃsummaries just won't be available for that query.?AIService also implements prompt engineering. When generating summaries, it doesn't just send the abstract to the model. It constructs a specific prompt: "Summarize the following research paper abstract in 2-3 sentences, focusing on the main contribution and findings. Abstract: {text}". This structured prompting produces more consistent results than raw text.PaperService handles paper metadata operations. Its main responsibility is deduplication, implemented in deduplicate(papers). This method takes a list of papers from multiple sources and figures out which ones are duplicates. The algorithm uses multiple signalsŃexact DOI matches, title similarity using Levenshtein distance (if titles are 90%+ similar and authors overlap, they're likely the same paper even without matching DOIs), arXiv ID matches since arXiv papers have unique identifiers that persist even when published elsewhere.When duplicates get identified, PaperService merges their metadata intelligently. It keeps the most complete information from each source. If one source has citation count and another has a full author list, the merged paper gets both. This produces richer metadata than any single source provides.PaperService also implements get_paper_details(id) which retrieves full information about a specific paper, potentially querying multiple providers to enrich metadata. And get_related_papers(id) which uses citation networks to find similar work.RankingService scores papers using a hybrid algorithm. The method rank(papers, query) takes a list of deduplicated papers and the original search query. It calculates a score for each paper based on multiple factors.Citation count mattersŃpapers with more citations score higher. The scoring uses logarithmic scaling because the difference between 10 and 20 citations matters more than between 1000 and 1010. Recency matters too. A paper from 2024 scores higher than one from 2015, all else equal. The boost decays exponentially with age. Source confidence is a factorŃresults from Semantic Scholar get higher weights than results from sources with less quality control. If AI scoring is enabled, AIService.score_relevance() contributes to the final score.Each factor gets a weight, and the final score is a weighted sum. Weights are configurable through a JSON file so ranking can be tuned without changing code. The default weights were determined empirically by testing with actual queries and seeing what produced useful result ordering.The service layer design follows established patterns. Services are statelessŃthey don't store data between method calls. They can be called from multiple threads safely. They handle errors by returning error objects rather than crashing. This makes the system robust and testable.3.3.3 Provider LayerProviders encapsulate external dependencies. AI Scholar follows a layered architecture based on the Model-View-Controller (MVC) pattern, adapted for a web application that integrates multiple external services.3.4.1 Overall Architecture The system is built in 4 layers. At the top is the Presentation Layer the web interface and REST API controllers. This is what users interact with. It displays search results, renders pages, handles API requests.Below that is the Service Layer where business logic lives. SearchService, AIService, PaperService, RankingServiceŃall the core functionality sits here. This layer doesn't care about HTTP or web stuff. It's just Python classes with methods that do the actual work.The Provider Layer handles external communication. Every call to arXiv's API, every query to Semantic Scholar, every request to language models goes through a provider. Each provider takes whatever format the external service uses and converts it to AI Scholar's internal data format.At the bottom is the Data LayerŃthe database and data models. PostgreSQL in production, SQLite for local development. This is where user accounts get stored, search history gets saved, results get cached.Data flows top to bottom and back up again. User submits a search. Request hits SearchController. Controller validates the input, calls SearchService. SearchService does the coordination asks each provider to query its database, waits for responses, calls PaperService to remove duplicates, calls RankingService to score and sort everything. Results come back up through SearchService to SearchController, which wraps them in JSON and sends to the browser.The architecture follows Model-View-Controller adapted for web applications. Controllers handle HTTP requests. Services contain the business logicŃthat's the model layer. Views are split between server-side templates for HTML pages and client-side JavaScript for dynamic behavior. Each piece has one job.Communication happens through method calls going down the stack. Controllers import services and call their methods. Services import providers and call their methods. The dependency only goes one directionŃproviders don't know about services; services don't know about controllers. This prevents circular dependencies and keeps things manageable.Testing gets easier with this structure. Each layer can be tested separately. Controllers get tested by making HTTP requests and checking responses. Services get tested by calling methods with mock data. Providers get tested by mocking external APIs. Then integration tests verify the layers work together.The design pattern is a standard layered architecture that's been used in web applications for years. Layers are clearly separated. Dependencies point one direction. Each component has a specific job. When something breaks, it's usually obvious which layer has the problem.3.4.2 Component InteractionComponents communicate through well-defined interfaces. Controllers talk to services. Services talk to providers and sometimes to other services. Providers talk to external APIs. Dependencies go one directionŃdown the stack. This keeps things clean.When SearchService needs to query databases, it doesn't just loop through providers one by one. It uses Python's asyncio to create asynchronous tasks. Each task calls one provider's search() method. All five tasks run at the same time instead of waiting for each to finish. SearchService waits for all tasks to complete, or times out after 5 seconds if something's slow. Results from all providers get collected into one list.Here's what the interaction looks like. SearchController receives a request and calls SearchService.search(query, options). SearchService looks at which databases are selected and creates a task for eachŃArxivProvider.search(query), SemanticScholarProvider.search(query), and so on. These execute in parallel. As each provider finishes, SearchService collects the results.Once all providers respond (or the timeout hits), SearchService has a list of papers from multiple sources. Many are duplicatesŃsame paper from different databases. It calls PaperService.deduplicate(papers) which returns the cleaned list. Then it calls RankingService.rank(papers, query) to score each paper and sort by relevance. If AI features are enabled, it calls AIService.generate_summaries(papers) for the top results. This happens asynchronously so the method returns immediately while summaries generate in the background.Error handling works in layers. Providers catch exceptions from external APIs and return empty result sets instead of letting errors propagate up. If arXiv times out, ArxivProvider logs the error and returns an empty list. SearchService gets results from the other four providers and continues. Services check for problems and handle them gracefully. If every single provider fails, SearchService returns an empty result set with metadata explaining what went wrong. Controllers catch exceptions from services and return proper HTTP error codesŃ400 for client problems, 500 for server issuesŃwith messages that make sense. This means one database failing doesn't crash the whole search.Services communicate through direct method calls. When SearchService needs deduplication, it instantiates PaperService and calls deduplicate(). When RankingService needs AI relevance scores, it creates an AIService instance and calls score_relevance(). Services don't keep permanent references to each otherŃthey create instances when needed. Keeps coupling loose.Caching happens in SearchService. It maintains an in-memory cache of recent searches, keyed by query string and selected options. Before querying providers, it checks the cache. If there's a matching entry that hasn't expired, it returns cached results immediately without hitting any APIs. Cache entries expire after 15 minutes because search results change as databases index new papers. The cache is just a Python dictionary with timestamps. It reduces API load and makes repeat searches fast.Data flows in a predictable sequence. User input comes through controllers. Controllers validate and pass to services. 5 Conclusions and Future Work5.1 IntroductionThis chapter assesses what the thesis achieved and where the work could go from here. The previous chapters laid out the database fragmentation problem, examined what others have done, designed AI Scholar, and built it. Now it needs to be evaluated whether it actually worked.The problem is straightforward enough. Doing a proper literature review means searching multiple databases because no single one has everything. Check arXiv for preprints, Semantic Scholar for citations, CrossRef for DOIs, CORE for repository papers, OpenAlex for broad coverage. Each one has different search syntax and returns results in different formats. Ending up running the same search five times, downloading results separately, then spending time figuring out which papers showed up in multiple places. That's time not spent actually reading papers.Chapter 2's literature review showed that current tools don't really fix this. Individual databases have gotten better at what they do. Semantic Scholar added AI features for finding related papers. But these all work independently. Researchers still have to manually coordinate searches and combine results themselves. The pieces needed to solve this existŃdatabase APIs, text processing algorithms, fuzzy matchingŃthey just haven't been put together in a way that helps with the actual workflow problem.Chapter 3 presented the architecture. It's a layered design where providers talk to individual databases, services do the real work (coordinating searches, removing duplicates, ranking results), and controllers handle what users see. The provider layer means if CrossRef changes their API, only modify that one module. Running queries asynchronously means hitting all five databases at once instead of one after another. Error handling lets the system keep working even when one database times out.Implementation got covered in Chapter 4. Python's asyncio was picked because it handles multiple API calls at the same time without blocking. Flask runs the web side. Deduplication matches DOIs first since those are exact, then compares titles using fuzzy matching for papers that don't have DOIs. Ranking uses citation counts (scaled logarithmically so one paper with 10,000 citations doesn't crush everything else), publication date (recent papers score higher), source reliability, and optionally AI relevance. Tests showed the pieces worked individually and together.Section 5.2 looks at whether AI Scholar did what it was supposed to do. The goals were: search multiple databases through one interface, remove duplicates automatically, rank results in a useful way, save researchers time. Did those things happen? The implementation and testing results answer the research questions from Chapter 1.Section 5.3 covers what comes next. Some features would make the system better but didn't fit in this thesis. Some parts could be improved based on what was learned while building it. This section lays out specific improvements that would make AI Scholar more useful or extend it to different research areas.The chapter wraps up by thinking about what this demonstrates more broadly. Can individual researchers build tools that actually fix gaps in current infrastructure? What does this say about whether it's realistic to integrate fragmented databases? Those questions matter for anyone else thinking about similar problems.5.2 ConclusionsThis thesis addressed a specific question: can integrating multiple scholarly databases through a single interface actually reduce the fragmentation problem that wastes researchers' time? The answer, based on what was built and tested, is yes.AI Scholar successfully integrated five major databasesŃarXiv, Semantic Scholar, CrossRef, CORE, and OpenAlex. A researcher types one query and gets results from all five sources. The asynchronous implementation keeps this fast. Instead of taking five separate searches at roughly 2 seconds each (10+ seconds total), aggregate searches finish in 3-5 seconds. That's because the system queries all providers simultaneously rather than waiting for each one sequentially. This alone saves time compared to manually visiting five different websites.The deduplication works. Testing with typical academic queries showed that when search the same terms across multiple databases, 15-25% of the results are duplicatesŃthe same paper appearing in different databases with slightly different metadata. AI Scholar's algorithm catches most of these. It matches DOIs exactly when they're available, which handles the majority of cases. For papers without DOIs (common with preprints), it uses fuzzy title matching combined with author comparison. The algorithm isn't perfectŃoccasionally it misses a duplicate or incorrectly merges two similar but distinct papersŃbut it catches enough duplicates to save researchers from manually checking dozens of entries.The ranking produces more useful results than just showing papers in whatever order each database returns them. Citation counts matter but get logarithmic scaling so one massively-cited paper doesn't dominate the top 20 results. Recent papers get a boost since researchers often care more about new work than old surveys. Source confidence adjusts for the fact that some databases have cleaner metadata than others. Combining these factors, highly-cited recent papers relevant to the query appear near the top, which is generally what researchers want to see first.The AI summarization helps, though with limits. Summaries let researchers quickly scan what a paper is about without reading the full abstract. For straightforward empirical papers, the summaries capture the main points reasonably well. For highly theoretical or mathematical papers, summaries sometimes miss nuances or oversimplify. Researchers still need to read papers themselves for anything important, but summaries speed up the initial filtering process.The three research questions from Chapter 1 can now be answered directly. First: can a unified interface reduce database fragmentation? YesŃquerying five databases through one interface is objectively simpler and faster than querying them separately. Second: how much do deduplication, ranking, and AI features improve discovery compared to searching databases individually? Deduplication eliminates 15-25% redundancy. Ranking orders results more usefully than arbitrary concatenation. AI summaries accelerate paper evaluation. Third: do researchers find this useful? The system does what it was designed to do and provides the intended functionality.Beyond answering the specific research questions, the work demonstrated something about research infrastructure more generally. Fixing this type of problem doesn't require breakthrough innovations or massive resources. The databases already exist with API access. Language models for summarization already exist. Fuzzy matching algorithms already exist. What was needed was integrationŃconnecting these pieces in a sensible way. One person working several months built a functional system. That suggests many research infrastructure problems are solvable at relatively small scale if someone actually builds the integration layer.The system has limitations that should be acknowledged. It's a research prototype running on free hosting, not production-grade software. The deduplication algorithm misses some duplicates and occasionally creates false matches. The ranking weights came from empirical testing rather than rigorous optimizationŃdifferent researchers might prefer different weightings. Some databases have rate limits that affect heavy usage. The interface works but isn't polished. These limitations don't prevent the system from being useful for its intended purpose, but they mean there's room for improvement.What matters is that AI Scholar proves the concept works. Multi-database integration is feasible. The core functionalityŃunified search, deduplication, intelligent rankingŃoperates as intended. Researchers can use this instead of manually coordinating searches across five separate platforms. The fragmentation problem that motivated this work hasn't been completely solved, but it's been reduced to something manageable.The thesis delivers concrete results. There's a working system that people can actually use. There's documentation of how it was built and why specific choices were made. There's evidence that the approach works based on implementation and testing. Future researchers can build on thisŃadd more databases, refine algorithms, adapt it for specific fields. The foundation exists.5.3 Future WorkSeveral directions would extend AI Scholar's capabilities and make it more useful for researchers. Some improvements are straightforward expansions of existing functionality. Others would require more substantial development effort but could significantly enhance the system's value.
